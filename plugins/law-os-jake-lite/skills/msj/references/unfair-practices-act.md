# MSJ Theory: Unfair Practices Act (UPA)

## Theory Code: UPA

## Governing Law

- **NMSA 1978, § 57-12-1 et seq.** — Unfair Practices Act
- **NMSA 1978, § 57-12-2** — Definitions (unfair or deceptive trade practice)
- **NMSA 1978, § 57-12-3** — Unlawful practices enumerated
- **NMSA 1978, § 57-12-10** — Private right of action; treble damages
- **Lohman v. Daimler-Chrysler Corp.**, 2007-NMCA-100 `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- **Stevenson v. Louis Dreyfus Corp.**, 1991-NMSC-051 `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## NM Standard

The UPA prohibits unfair or deceptive trade practices and unconscionable
trade practices in the conduct of trade or commerce. A private plaintiff
may recover actual damages or $100 (whichever is greater), plus treble
damages if the court finds the defendant willfully engaged in the practice.

The UPA is to be **liberally construed** to protect consumers. `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Element Checklist

### Element 1: Trade or Commerce
- Defendant engaged in "trade or commerce" as defined by § 57-12-2
- The transaction or conduct at issue was in the regular course of
  business or commerce
- Insurance transactions are trade or commerce under the UPA `[VERIFY-CITE]`

### Element 2: Unfair or Deceptive Practice
One or more of the practices enumerated in § 57-12-3, or the general
prohibition on unfair/deceptive practices:
- **Misrepresentation:** False or misleading statement of material fact
- **Omission:** Failure to disclose material information
- **Deceptive conduct:** Conduct likely to mislead a reasonable person
- **Unconscionable practice:** Taking advantage of consumer's lack of
  knowledge, ability, experience, or capacity (§ 57-12-2(E))

Specific UPA violations commonly at issue:
- § 57-12-3(B) — Causing confusion as to source, sponsorship, or affiliation
- § 57-12-3(D) — Representing goods/services have characteristics they do not
- § 57-12-3(E) — Representing goods/services are of a quality they are not
- § 57-12-3(N) — Using deception, fraud, false promise, or misrepresentation

### Element 3: Person Aggrieved / Standing
- Plaintiff suffered actual damage as a result of the unfair practice
- Plaintiff is a "person" within the meaning of the Act

### Element 4: Causation
- The unfair or deceptive practice caused or contributed to plaintiff's loss
- Reliance (for misrepresentation claims): plaintiff relied on the
  misrepresentation to their detriment `[VERIFY-CITE]`

### Element 5: Damages
- Actual damages (out-of-pocket loss, consequential damages)
- Treble damages if willful (§ 57-12-10(B)) — must show defendant's
  conduct was willful
- Attorney's fees and costs (§ 57-12-10(C))
- Minimum $100 if actual damages are less

## Burden of Proof

**Plaintiff bears the burden** on all elements. Willfulness (for treble
damages) must be proved by the plaintiff. `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## UPA in Insurance Context

The UPA is frequently paired with bad faith / unfair insurance practices
claims. Key interactions:
- Insurer's misrepresentation of policy terms = UPA violation
- Failure to disclose material coverage limitations = UPA omission
- UPA damages are in addition to breach of contract damages
- UPA treble damages and attorney's fees provide additional recovery

## Common Defense Arguments to Anticipate

- Conduct was not in "trade or commerce"
- No misrepresentation or deception occurred
- Plaintiff did not rely on the alleged misrepresentation
- No actual damages resulted
- Practice was not "willful" (defeats treble damages only)
- Regulatory compliance defense (conduct consistent with regulations)

## Key Evidence Types for SUMF

- Marketing materials, advertisements, representations made
- Contract terms vs. representations
- Correspondence (what was told to consumer)
- Consumer's actions in reliance on representations
- Documentation of actual damages
- Pattern evidence (same practice applied to others)
- Internal documents showing knowledge of deceptive nature

## SUMF Organization

1. Trade or commerce facts (business relationship)
2. Specific unfair or deceptive practice facts (what was said/done/omitted)
3. Reliance facts (what plaintiff did based on the practice)
4. Causation facts (how the practice caused loss)
5. Damages facts (actual loss quantified)
6. Willfulness facts (if seeking treble damages)
