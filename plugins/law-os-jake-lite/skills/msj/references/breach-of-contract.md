# MSJ Theory: Breach of Contract

## Theory Code: BREACH-CONTRACT

## Governing Law

- **UJI 13-822 NMRA** — Breach of contract instruction `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- **Camino Real Mobile Home Park P'ship v. Wolfe**, 1995-NMSC-013 `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- NM common law on contract formation, performance, breach, and damages

## NM Standard

A breach of contract claim requires proof that: (1) a valid and binding
contract existed; (2) plaintiff performed or was excused from performance;
(3) defendant failed to perform a material obligation under the contract;
and (4) plaintiff suffered damages as a result. `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Element Checklist

### Element 1: Existence of a Valid Contract
- Offer and acceptance (meeting of the minds)
- Consideration (mutual exchange of value)
- Definite and certain terms (essential terms identifiable)
- Capacity of parties to contract
- Identify the specific contract (written or oral; date; parties)
- If written: attach or reference the contract with Bates/exhibit cite

### Element 2: Material Terms
- Identify the specific contractual obligations at issue
- Quote or cite the relevant contract provisions (with pinpoint to
  section/paragraph)
- For insurance contracts: identify the policy, endorsements, declarations
- Interpretation: NM follows plain meaning; ambiguity construed against
  drafter `[VERIFY-CITE]`

### Element 3: Plaintiff's Performance (or Excuse)
- Plaintiff performed all conditions precedent
- Plaintiff fulfilled all material obligations under the contract
- OR plaintiff was excused from performance (waiver, impossibility,
  anticipatory breach by defendant, etc.)

### Element 4: Defendant's Breach
- Defendant failed to perform a material obligation
- Identify the specific obligation(s) breached
- Evidence of non-performance (correspondence, failure to pay, failure
  to provide coverage, failure to deliver services)
- Materiality: the breach goes to the essence of the contract, not a
  minor or technical failure

### Element 5: Causation
- Defendant's breach caused plaintiff's damages
- But-for the breach, plaintiff would not have suffered the loss

### Element 6: Damages
- Expectation damages (put plaintiff in position if contract performed)
- Consequential damages (foreseeable at time of contract formation)
- Incidental damages (costs of mitigation)
- Duty to mitigate applies
- UPA damages may also apply if breach involves deceptive practices

## Burden of Proof

**Plaintiff bears the burden** on all elements by preponderance of evidence.

For contract interpretation: if the contract is unambiguous, interpretation
is a question of law for the court (favorable for MSJ). If ambiguous,
interpretation is a question of fact (disfavors MSJ unless extrinsic
evidence is undisputed). `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Insurance Contract Specifics

For breach of insurance contract claims:
- **Duty to defend:** Insurer's duty to defend is broader than duty to
  indemnify; triggered by allegations in the complaint `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- **Duty to indemnify:** Determined by actual facts, not allegations
- **Reservation of rights:** Insurer's reservation does not relieve
  duty to defend
- **Bad faith overlay:** Breach + unreasonable conduct may support
  separate bad faith claim

## Common Defense Arguments to Anticipate

- No valid contract existed (formation defects)
- Plaintiff breached first (prior material breach)
- Conditions precedent not satisfied
- The breach was not material
- Damages not caused by the breach
- Failure to mitigate damages
- Contract defenses (statute of frauds, unconscionability, illegality)
- For insurance: policy exclusion, late notice, misrepresentation on application

## Key Evidence Types for SUMF

- The contract itself (with relevant provisions highlighted)
- Evidence of plaintiff's performance (payments, compliance, notice)
- Evidence of defendant's non-performance (correspondence, claims files)
- Demand letters and responses
- Damages documentation (invoices, expert reports, lost profits analysis)
- For insurance: the policy, endorsements, claim file, denial letters

## SUMF Organization

1. Contract formation facts (who, when, what contract)
2. Material terms at issue (quoted provisions)
3. Plaintiff's performance facts
4. Defendant's breach facts (specific failures)
5. Causation facts
6. Damages facts (quantified with sources)
