# MSJ Theory: Defeating Opponent's Affirmative Defenses

## Theory Code: DEFEAT-AFF-DEF

## Governing Law

- **NMRA 1-056** — Summary judgment standard
- **NMRA 1-008(C)** — Affirmative defenses must be pleaded
- **NMRA 1-008(A)** — Defenses must contain short, plain statement of facts
- **Celotex Corp. v. Catrett**, 477 U.S. 317 (1986) — MSJ burden framework `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- NM case law on burden allocation for affirmative defenses `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## NM Standard

Plaintiff may move for partial summary judgment to eliminate one or more
of defendant's affirmative defenses. Because the defendant bears the
burden of proof on affirmative defenses, plaintiff's MSJ burden is to
show that the defendant **cannot establish** an essential element of the
defense — either because the undisputed facts negate the defense, or
because the defendant lacks evidence to support it.

This is a powerful offensive tool: eliminating affirmative defenses
before trial narrows the issues and prevents jury confusion from
unsupported defenses.

## How This Theory Differs

Unlike other MSJ theories where plaintiff proves elements of a claim,
here plaintiff attacks elements of a defense. The framework inverts:

- **Plaintiff's MSJ burden:** Show defendant cannot prove an element of
  the affirmative defense
- **If met:** Burden shifts to defendant to show genuine dispute on that element
- **If not met:** Defense survives to trial

## Element Checklist (General Framework)

For each affirmative defense to be defeated:

### Step 1: Identify the Defense
- What affirmative defense did defendant plead? (NMRA 1-008(C))
- What are the legal elements of that defense?
- Load the specific defense elements from LPB

### Step 2: Identify the Weakest Element
- Which element of the defense lacks evidentiary support?
- Which element is negated by undisputed record facts?
- Target the element where defendant's proof is thinnest

### Step 3: Prove the Element Cannot Be Established
Either:
- **Negation:** Undisputed facts affirmatively negate an element
  (e.g., comparative fault defense negated by showing defendant was
  100% at fault as a matter of law)
- **Absence of evidence:** Defendant has no evidence to support an element
  after adequate time for discovery (cite Celotex framework)

### Step 4: Demonstrate No Genuine Dispute
- The evidence, viewed in light most favorable to defendant, does not
  create a genuine dispute on the targeted element
- Mere allegations or speculation are insufficient

## Common Affirmative Defenses to Target

### Comparative Fault (NMSA § 41-3A-1)
**Elements defendant must prove:**
- Plaintiff's conduct fell below reasonable care
- Plaintiff's conduct was a cause of plaintiff's injuries
- Percentage allocation

**Attack vectors:**
- No evidence plaintiff did anything wrong
- Plaintiff's alleged conduct was not a proximate cause
- Defendant's own evidence negates comparative fault

### Assumption of Risk
**Elements defendant must prove:**
- Plaintiff knew of the specific risk
- Plaintiff voluntarily encountered the risk
- Plaintiff appreciated the nature and extent of the danger

**Attack vectors:**
- No evidence plaintiff knew of the specific risk that materialized
- Risk was not open and obvious
- Plaintiff had no meaningful choice (involuntary exposure)

### Statute of Limitations
**Elements defendant must prove:**
- Claim accrued before the limitations period
- No tolling doctrine applies

**Attack vectors:**
- Discovery rule: plaintiff did not know and could not reasonably have
  known of the injury or its cause until within the limitations period
- Tolling (minority, incapacity, fraudulent concealment)
- Continuing tort doctrine

### Failure to Mitigate
**Elements defendant must prove:**
- Plaintiff failed to take reasonable steps to minimize damages
- Reasonable mitigation would have reduced damages by a specific amount

**Attack vectors:**
- Plaintiff's actions were reasonable under the circumstances
- No evidence of what specific mitigation steps should have been taken
- Defendant cannot quantify what mitigation would have saved

### Consent / Release / Waiver
**Elements defendant must prove:**
- Valid consent, release, or waiver existed
- It covers the specific claims at issue
- It was knowing and voluntary

**Attack vectors:**
- Release is unconscionable or obtained through fraud/duress
- Release does not cover the specific claims at issue (narrow construction)
- Lack of consideration
- Public policy limitations on releases `[VERIFY-CITE]`

### Pre-Existing Condition / Eggshell Plaintiff
**Note:** This is NOT a valid defense in NM — the eggshell plaintiff
doctrine means defendant takes plaintiff as found. `[VERIFY-CITE]`
If defendant pleads this, move to strike under NMRA 1-012(F) via ADR
skill, or defeat via MSJ showing it is not a recognized defense.

## Burden of Proof

**Defendant bears the burden** on affirmative defenses.

**Plaintiff's MSJ burden:** Show that defendant cannot meet their burden
on at least one essential element of the defense. This can be met by:
1. Pointing to absence of evidence supporting the element, OR
2. Presenting undisputed facts that negate the element

## Key Evidence Types for SUMF

- Discovery responses showing defendant's evidence (or lack thereof)
- Defendant's deposition testimony on the defense
- Expert reports (or absence of expert support for defense)
- Documents negating the defense elements
- Interrogatory answers identifying defendant's evidence for each defense

## SUMF Organization

For each targeted affirmative defense:
1. Facts establishing what defendant pleaded
2. Facts negating the defense element(s) OR showing absence of evidence
3. Facts showing no genuine dispute exists
