# MSJ Theory: Medical Malpractice — Duty to Inform (Informed Consent)

## Theory Code: MED-MAL-INFORM

## Governing Law

- **NMSA 1978, § 41-4-26** — Informed consent statute (government healthcare providers)
- **Gerety v. Demers**, 1978-NMSC-016 — Established patient-centered standard for informed consent in NM
- **UJI 13-1101 NMRA** — Medical malpractice instruction (general)

## NM Standard

New Mexico follows the **patient-centered (materiality) standard**, not the
physician-centered standard. The question is what a reasonable patient would
want to know, not what a reasonable physician would disclose.

A physician must disclose information that a **reasonable patient** would
consider material to the decision whether to consent to the proposed
treatment or procedure. `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Element Checklist

Draft the Argument section with one sub-section per element:

### Element 1: Provider-Patient Relationship
- Defendant owed a duty of care to plaintiff as treating provider
- Establish the treatment relationship (dates, facility, specialty)

### Element 2: Proposed Treatment or Procedure
- Identify the specific treatment, procedure, or intervention at issue
- What the provider recommended or performed

### Element 3: Material Risk Not Disclosed
- Identify the specific risk(s) that materialized
- A reasonable patient would have considered this risk material to the
  decision whether to consent
- Provider failed to disclose this material risk before obtaining consent
- Use medical records to show what was/was not documented as disclosed

### Element 4: Reasonable Patient Standard
- A reasonable patient in plaintiff's position, if informed of the
  undisclosed risk, would not have consented to the treatment
- Distinguish from hindsight — focus on what was knowable before treatment

### Element 5: Causation — Risk Materialized
- The undisclosed risk actually materialized
- The treatment or procedure caused the injury complained of
- Requires medical evidence linking treatment to outcome

### Element 6: Damages
- Physical injury, pain and suffering, medical expenses, lost wages
- UJI 13-1802 through 13-1810A categories apply
- Medical specials = amounts billed (firm rule)

## Burden of Proof

**Plaintiff bears the burden** on all elements.

For Element 4 (reasonable patient standard), this is an objective test —
would a reasonable patient have declined? Not what this particular
plaintiff subjectively would have done. `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Common Defense Arguments to Anticipate

- Patient was informed (documentation in consent forms)
- Risk was not material / was too remote
- Patient would have consented anyway (causation failure)
- Emergency exception (NMSA § 41-4-26 exception for emergencies)
- Therapeutic privilege (rare; disfavored in NM)

## Key Evidence Types for SUMF

- Medical records (consent forms, operative notes, pre-op documentation)
- Deposition testimony of provider re: disclosure practices
- Expert testimony on materiality of risk and standard of care
- Patient testimony re: what was/was not communicated
- Statistical data on risk incidence

## SUMF Organization

Group SUMF paragraphs by element:
1. Treatment relationship facts
2. What procedure was proposed/performed
3. What risks were/were not disclosed (records-based)
4. What a reasonable patient would consider material
5. What injury occurred and its connection to the undisclosed risk
6. Damages facts
