# MSJ Theory: Medical Malpractice — Duty to Transfer

## Theory Code: MED-MAL-TRANSFER

## Governing Law

- **UJI 13-1101 NMRA** — Medical malpractice instruction (general)
- **NMSA 1978, § 41-5-1 et seq.** — Medical Malpractice Act
- **EMTALA (42 U.S.C. § 1395dd)** — Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act (federal; applies to hospitals receiving Medicare)
- NM case law on duty to transfer when care exceeds facility capability `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## NM Standard

A healthcare provider has a duty to transfer a patient to an appropriate
facility when:
1. The patient's condition requires care beyond the provider's capability
   or the facility's resources, AND
2. A reasonably prudent provider would recognize the need for transfer

The duty arises when the provider knows or should know that continued
treatment at the current facility poses an unreasonable risk to the
patient. `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Element Checklist

### Element 1: Provider-Patient Relationship / Duty of Care
- Defendant provider or facility owed a duty of care
- Establish the treatment relationship and facility capabilities

### Element 2: Condition Requiring Transfer
- Patient's condition required care beyond the facility's capability
- Identify the specific medical need that exceeded available resources
- Use records showing diagnoses, complications, or deterioration

### Element 3: Knowledge (Actual or Constructive)
- Provider knew or should have known of the need for transfer
- Evidence: lab results, imaging, vital signs, nursing notes, consultations
- A reasonably prudent provider in the same specialty would have recognized
  the need for transfer

### Element 4: Failure to Transfer
- Provider failed to initiate or complete transfer to an appropriate facility
- Delay in transfer decision or execution
- Failure to arrange appropriate transport (level of care during transport)

### Element 5: Appropriate Facility Available
- An appropriate receiving facility existed and was available
- The receiving facility had the capability to provide the needed care
- Transfer was feasible given the patient's condition

### Element 6: Causation
- The failure to transfer (or delay in transfer) caused or contributed to
  plaintiff's injuries
- Earlier transfer would have resulted in a different outcome
- Typically requires expert testimony on causation

### Element 7: Damages
- Physical injury, worsened condition, additional treatment needed
- Pain and suffering during delay
- Medical expenses (amounts billed)
- UJI 13-1802 through 13-1810A categories

## Burden of Proof

**Plaintiff bears the burden** on all elements, including expert testimony
on standard of care and causation. NMSA § 41-5-1 et seq. governs
procedural requirements (medical review panel if applicable). `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## EMTALA Considerations (Federal)

If the facility receives Medicare and the patient presented to the
emergency department:
- EMTALA requires a medical screening examination
- If an emergency medical condition exists, the facility must stabilize
  OR transfer with informed consent and appropriate transport
- EMTALA provides a private right of action
- EMTALA sets a floor; NM common law may impose additional duties

## Common Defense Arguments to Anticipate

- Facility had adequate capability to treat
- Transfer was medically contraindicated (patient too unstable)
- Patient refused transfer
- No receiving facility available or willing
- Delay did not cause the injury (would have occurred regardless)

## Key Evidence Types for SUMF

- Medical records (progress notes, nursing notes, vital signs trending)
- Transfer documentation (or absence thereof)
- Facility capability records (staffing, equipment, specialty availability)
- Expert testimony on standard of care for transfer decisions
- Communication records between facilities
- EMTALA logs (if applicable)

## SUMF Organization

1. Treatment relationship and facility facts
2. Patient condition facts (chronological deterioration)
3. Provider knowledge facts (what they knew and when)
4. Transfer failure facts (what was/was not done)
5. Available facility facts
6. Causation facts (expert opinions, outcome comparison)
7. Damages facts
