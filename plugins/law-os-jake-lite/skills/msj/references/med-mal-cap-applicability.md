# MSJ Theory: Medical Malpractice Act Cap Applicability

## Theory Code: MED-MAL-CAP

## Governing Law

- **NMSA 1978, § 41-5-3** — Definitions (qualified healthcare provider, malpractice)
- **NMSA 1978, § 41-5-5** — Patient's compensation fund

## Purpose of This Theory

Plaintiff seeks a **threshold ruling** that certain claims are **not subject
to the MMA cap** — either because they fall outside the MMA's definition of
"malpractice," or because the defendant is not a "qualified healthcare provider,"
or because the claims sound in a different legal theory that is not governed
by the MMA.

This is a critical strategic motion: if successful, it removes the statutory
cap and opens full common-law damages recovery on those claims.

## NM Standard

The MMA applies to claims of "malpractice" against "qualified healthcare
providers." If a claim does not meet both requirements, the MMA cap does
not apply and the claim proceeds under common law without limitation.

**Key definitions (§ 41-5-3):**
- **"Malpractice"**: Failure to exercise the degree of care recognized as
  acceptable and appropriate by reasonably prudent similar healthcare
  providers `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- **"Qualified healthcare provider"**: Provider who has filed proof of
  financial responsibility with the Superintendent of Insurance and is
  covered by the Patient's Compensation Fund `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Element Checklist

### Path A: Claim Is Not "Malpractice" Under the MMA

#### Element A1: Identify the Claim
- State the specific legal theory (e.g., battery, fraud, breach of contract,
  UPA, premises liability, negligent supervision of non-medical staff)
- Distinguish from professional medical judgment

#### Element A2: Claim Does Not Arise from Medical Care
The claim does not involve the exercise of medical judgment, skill, or
knowledge. Examples of non-malpractice claims against healthcare entities:

- **Premises liability:** Slip/fall at hospital, parking lot conditions,
  facility maintenance failures
- **Battery:** Treatment without any consent (not informed consent — which
  is malpractice) `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- **Fraud/misrepresentation:** Fraudulent billing, false statements about
  qualifications, misrepresentation of treatment outcomes
- **UPA violations:** Deceptive advertising of services, false quality claims
- **Breach of contract:** Non-medical contractual obligations
- **Negligent hiring/supervision:** Of non-medical staff (security, janitorial,
  administrative) where the negligence is not in medical judgment
- **Negligent credentialing:** Failure to verify provider qualifications
  (administrative, not medical, decision) `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- **Corporate negligence:** Systemic institutional failures (understaffing,
  equipment maintenance, policy failures) `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- **Statutory claims:** Civil rights violations, ADA violations, EMTALA

#### Element A3: Scope of "Malpractice" — The Dividing Line
The NM courts apply a **substance-of-the-claim** test: look at what the
claim actually alleges, not what the plaintiff labels it.

- If the claim requires proof of deviation from medical standard of care →
  it is malpractice regardless of label
- If the claim can be proved without reference to medical standard of care →
  it is not malpractice and the cap does not apply

`[VERIFY-CITE]` for current NM holdings on the substance-of-the-claim test.

### Path B: Defendant Is Not a "Qualified Healthcare Provider"

#### Element B1: Qualification Status
- Defendant has not filed proof of financial responsibility with the
  Superintendent of Insurance
- Defendant is not covered by the Patient's Compensation Fund
- Verification: check with the Superintendent of Insurance `[VERIFY]`

#### Element B2: Consequence of Non-Qualification
If the defendant is not a qualified healthcare provider:
- The MMA cap does not apply
- The MMA medical review panel requirement may not apply `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- The claim proceeds under common law

### Path C: Mixed Claims (Some Capped, Some Not)

#### Element C1: Identify Separable Claims
- Some claims against the same defendant may be malpractice (capped)
  while others are non-malpractice (uncapped)
- The court can rule on each claim separately

#### Element C2: Prevent Improper Consolidation
- Defendant may argue all claims are "really" malpractice to bring
  everything under the cap
- Plaintiff must show each non-malpractice claim is independently viable
  without reference to medical standard of care

## Burden of Proof

**Plaintiff bears the burden** of showing the claim falls outside the MMA
(for Path A or Path C).

**Defendant bears the burden** of showing the MMA applies (to invoke the
cap as an affirmative defense). `[VERIFY-CITE]`

For **Path B** (non-qualified provider): Plaintiff can establish this through
public records (Superintendent of Insurance filings).

## Strategic Considerations

- **File this motion early.** A ruling on cap applicability shapes the entire
  case valuation and settlement posture.
- **Pair with declaratory judgment** if the coverage question is complex.
- **Discovery on qualification status:** Request defendant's proof of
  financial responsibility filing; if not filed, cap argument collapses.
- **Multiple defendants:** Some may be qualified, others not. Cap applies
  per-defendant based on qualification status.

## Common Defense Arguments to Anticipate

- All claims arise from medical care (substance-of-the-claim test)
- Corporate negligence is really malpractice in disguise
- Claims are inextricably intertwined with medical judgment
- Defendant is a qualified healthcare provider (proof of filing)
- The MMA preempts common law claims against healthcare providers

## Key Evidence Types for SUMF

- Defendant's MMA qualification filing (or proof of non-filing)
- Nature of the claims as pleaded
- Facts showing the claim does/does not require medical standard of care proof
- Expert disclosures (if claim can be proved without medical expert → supports
  non-malpractice characterization)
- Superintendent of Insurance records
- Facility licensing and credentialing records
- Employment records for non-medical staff (if negligent hiring/supervision)

## SUMF Organization

**Path A:**
1. Facts establishing the specific claim and its legal theory
2. Facts showing the claim does not arise from medical judgment
3. Facts showing the claim can be proved without reference to medical
   standard of care

**Path B:**
1. Facts identifying the defendant as a healthcare entity/provider
2. Facts showing defendant's MMA qualification status (or lack thereof)

**Path C:**
1. Facts separating malpractice claims from non-malpractice claims
2. Facts showing each non-malpractice claim is independently viable
