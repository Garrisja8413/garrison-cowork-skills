# MSJ Theory: Medical Malpractice — Duty to Maintain Records

## Theory Code: MED-MAL-RECORDS

## Governing Law

- **NMSA 1978, § 14-6-2** — Health information; access; patient's rights
- **NMAC 7.1.2** — Medical records regulation `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- **UJI 13-1101 NMRA** — Medical malpractice instruction
- **NMSA 1978, § 41-5-1 et seq.** — Medical Malpractice Act
- NM case law on spoliation inference from missing/altered records `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- Federal: 42 CFR Part 482 (hospital conditions of participation — record-keeping)

## NM Standard

Healthcare providers have a duty to create and maintain accurate, complete,
and contemporaneous medical records. This duty serves both treatment
continuity and the patient's right to access records of their care.

Failure to maintain records may give rise to:
1. Direct negligence (substandard record-keeping as breach of duty)
2. Spoliation inference (if records are missing/altered, may infer contents
   were unfavorable to the provider)
3. Impairment of patient's ability to prove other malpractice claims

## Element Checklist

### Element 1: Duty to Maintain Records
- Provider had a duty to create and maintain accurate medical records
- Statutory and regulatory basis for the duty
- Standard of care for record-keeping in the relevant specialty

### Element 2: Breach — Deficient Record-Keeping
One or more of:
- **Missing records:** Records that should exist are absent (e.g., operative
  notes, consent forms, nursing assessments, medication administration records)
- **Altered records:** Evidence of post-hoc alterations (metadata analysis,
  late entries without proper documentation, inconsistencies)
- **Incomplete records:** Key information omitted (vital signs gaps, missing
  assessment entries, unsigned orders)
- **Inaccurate records:** Documentation contradicts contemporaneous evidence
  (other provider notes, imaging, test results)

### Element 3: Causation — Connection to Harm
The records deficiency either:
- **Directly caused harm:** Treatment decisions were based on inaccurate
  records (wrong medication, missed diagnosis due to missing history)
- **Impaired continuity of care:** Subsequent providers lacked information
  needed for proper treatment
- **Impaired plaintiff's ability to prove claims:** Missing records make it
  impossible to establish what happened (spoliation theory)

### Element 4: Spoliation Inference (if applicable)
- Records were in the exclusive control of the defendant
- Records were lost, destroyed, or altered after the duty to preserve arose
- The missing records are relevant to plaintiff's claims
- Inference: contents of missing records were unfavorable to defendant

### Element 5: Damages
- Physical harm from treatment errors caused by deficient records
- Inability to prove the full extent of other malpractice claims
- Additional medical expenses, pain and suffering
- UJI 13-1802 through 13-1810A categories

## Burden of Proof

**Plaintiff bears the initial burden** on elements 1-3.

For **spoliation** (Element 4), once plaintiff shows records are missing
and were in defendant's control, the burden may shift to defendant to
explain the absence. The court has discretion on whether to instruct the
jury on spoliation inference. `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Common Defense Arguments to Anticipate

- Records are complete and accurate
- Missing records were lost due to system failure (not intentional)
- Records deficiency did not cause harm (treatment was proper regardless)
- Standard of care for record-keeping was met
- Statute of limitations on records claims

## Key Evidence Types for SUMF

- Medical records (with gaps, inconsistencies, or alterations identified)
- Metadata analysis of electronic health records (edit history, timestamps)
- Deposition testimony of records custodians
- Expert testimony on record-keeping standards
- Regulatory requirements for the facility type
- Audit trails from EHR systems
- Comparison records from other providers (showing what should have been documented)

## SUMF Organization

1. Provider relationship and record-keeping duty facts
2. Specific records deficiencies (missing, altered, incomplete, inaccurate)
3. Evidence of alteration or spoliation (if applicable)
4. How deficiency caused or contributed to harm
5. Damages facts
