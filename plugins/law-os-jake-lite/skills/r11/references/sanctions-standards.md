## The Four Certification Obligations

### 1. Improper Purpose — Rule 11(b)(1) / NMRA 1-011(A)(1)

**Standard:** The pleading is not presented for any improper purpose, such as
to harass, cause unnecessary delay, or needlessly increase the cost of
litigation. [VERIFY-CITE]

**Test:** Objective — but courts also consider subjective indications of purpose.
The inquiry asks whether the filing is objectively baseless AND filed for an
improper purpose. [VERIFY-CITE]

**Proving improper purpose — objective indicators:**
- Pattern of baseless filings or contentions designed to impose costs
- Filing motions solely to delay proceedings
- Asserting claims or defenses known to be meritless to extract settlement
  pressure
- Refusing to withdraw contentions after their baselessness is demonstrated
- Multiplying proceedings on matters that should be undisputed

**Key distinction:** Improper purpose is the only basis under FRCP 11 that
allows monetary sanctions against a represented party (not just counsel).
FRCP 11(c)(5)(A). [VERIFY-CITE]

**Difficulty level:** Highest. Courts are reluctant to find improper purpose
absent a clear pattern of bad-faith litigation conduct. Include only with
strong record support and senior attorney approval.

### 2. Legal Contentions Not Warranted — Rule 11(b)(2) / NMRA 1-011(A)(2)

**Standard:** The claims, defenses, and other legal contentions are warranted
by existing law or by a nonfrivolous argument for extending, modifying, or
reversing existing law or for establishing new law. [VERIFY-CITE]

**Test:** Objective reasonableness — would a competent attorney, after
reasonable legal research, have concluded that the legal contention has a
plausible basis? [VERIFY-CITE]

**What satisfies the standard (NOT sanctionable):**
- Legal contention supported by binding authority
- Legal contention supported by persuasive authority from other jurisdictions
- Contention that existing law should be modified, with a nonfrivolous
  argument explaining why
- Novel legal theory supported by a reasonable extension of existing principles
- Good faith argument based on ambiguity in the governing standard

**What fails the standard (sanctionable):**
- Legal contention directly contradicted by binding precedent with no argument
  for distinguishing or overruling
- Defense foreclosed by the plain text of a governing statute or rule
- Legal position abandoned or withdrawn by the party in prior proceedings
- Contention based on a law that has been repealed or superseded
- Assertion of a defense that is legally inapplicable to the claims at issue
  (e.g., asserting comparative fault in a strict liability claim where
  comparative fault is not a defense) [VERIFY-CITE]

**Proof structure:**
1. Identify the specific legal contention
2. Cite the binding authority that forecloses it
3. Show no nonfrivolous basis for modification or extension
4. Show that reasonable research would have revealed the deficiency

### 3. Factual Contentions Without Support — Rule 11(b)(3) / NMRA 1-011(A)(3)

**Standard:** The factual contentions have evidentiary support or, if
specifically so identified, will likely have evidentiary support after a
reasonable opportunity for further investigation or discovery. [VERIFY-CITE]

**Test:** Objective — did the attorney conduct a reasonable factual inquiry
before presenting the contention? Did the contention have any evidentiary
basis at the time of filing? [VERIFY-CITE]

**"Specifically so identified" exception:**
- A party may present a factual contention that does not yet have evidentiary
  support IF the contention is specifically identified as one likely to have
  support after investigation or discovery [VERIFY-CITE]
- The contention must be plausible on its face
- There must be a reasonable basis to believe evidence will be found
- This exception applies to initial pleadings more generously than to
  amended pleadings or motions filed after discovery

**What fails the standard:**
- Factual contentions contradicted by the filing party's own documents
- Factual contentions contradicted by the filing party's own prior sworn
  testimony or discovery responses
- Factual assertions with no evidentiary basis that are not identified as
  requiring further investigation
- Factual predicates for defenses that the party knows or should know are
  false based on its own records

**Proof structure:**
1. Identify the specific factual contention in the pleading
2. Cite the record evidence contradicting it (with Bates/pinpoints)
3. Show the contradicting evidence was available to the filing party
4. Demonstrate that reasonable inquiry would have revealed the lack of support

---

### 4. Denials Not Warranted — Rule 11(b)(4) / NMRA 1-011(A)(4)

**Standard:** The denials of factual contentions are warranted on the evidence
or, if specifically so identified, are reasonably based on belief or a lack
of information. [VERIFY-CITE]

**Test:** Objective — did the attorney investigate the facts before denying
them? Is there an evidentiary basis for the denial? [VERIFY-CITE]

**What fails the standard:**
- Blanket denial of facts established by the denying party's own records
- Denial of facts that the party personally witnessed or participated in
- "Lacks knowledge or information" response regarding facts within the party's
  control or readily accessible through the party's own records
- Denial of facts admitted by the same party in other filings or discovery
  in the same case
- Blanket denial of multi-fact paragraphs containing clearly undeniable facts
  (dates, locations, document existence)

**Cross-reference with ADR flags:**
- `BLANKET-DENIAL` → candidate for Rule 11(b)(4) when the denied facts are
  within the party's knowledge
- `LKI-IMPROPER` → candidate for Rule 11(b)(4) when facts are within the
  party's control
- `EVASIVE` → candidate for Rule 11(b)(4) when the evasion masks a failure
  to investigate
- `INTERNAL-CONFLICT` → candidate for Rule 11(b)(4) when the denial
  contradicts the party's own affirmative defense

**Proof structure:**
1. Identify the specific denial
2. Cite the averment that was denied
3. Show the evidence that makes the denial unwarranted (party's own records,
   sworn testimony, prior admissions)
4. Demonstrate that reasonable inquiry would have revealed the denial lacks
   evidentiary basis

## Pattern vs. Isolated Instance

### Why Pattern Matters

Courts are reluctant to impose sanctions for a single pleading deficiency,
recognizing that reasonable attorneys can make good-faith errors. [VERIFY-CITE]

A pattern of certification violations is far more persuasive because it:
- Demonstrates systemic failure of the reasonable inquiry obligation
- Eliminates the "honest mistake" defense
- Shows the conduct is not self-correcting
- Justifies the deterrence rationale for sanctions

### Establishing a Pattern

**Quantitative indicators:**
- 3+ denials of facts within the party's own knowledge/records
- 5+ blanket denials of multi-fact paragraphs without specificity
- 2+ defenses foreclosed by binding authority
- 10+ defective responses (any type) suggesting no review of the complaint

**Qualitative indicators:**
- Same deficiency type repeated across multiple paragraphs or defenses
- Deficiencies targeting the core elements of the plaintiff's claims
- Deficiencies that the party could not reasonably have overlooked
  (denying facts from the party's own documents)
- Failure to cure after notice (safe harbor notice, meet-and-confer letter)

### Single-Instance Exception

A single instance may warrant sanctions when:
- The contention is so clearly baseless that no reasonable attorney would
  present it (e.g., asserting a defense explicitly repealed by statute)
- The single contention caused significant, concrete harm to the opposing
  party (e.g., necessitated extensive discovery on a baseless issue)
- The filing party persisted in the contention after its baselessness was
  demonstrated (failure to cure during safe harbor period)

When relying on a single instance, the motion must explicitly address why
the single violation warrants sanctions despite the general preference for
pattern-based sanctions.

## Sanctions Factors

### Factors Courts Consider in Determining Sanctions

1. **Severity of the violation** — how far the conduct departs from the
   certification standard [VERIFY-CITE]
2. **Number of violations** — pattern vs. isolated instance [VERIFY-CITE]
3. **Impact on the litigation** — how much the conduct increased costs, delayed
   proceedings, or prejudiced the opposing party [VERIFY-CITE]
4. **Willfulness** — whether the violation was correctable and the party
   failed to correct after notice [VERIFY-CITE]
5. **Prior sanctions history** — whether the attorney or party has been
   sanctioned before [VERIFY-CITE]
6. **Deterrence need** — what sanction suffices to deter repetition
   [VERIFY-CITE]
7. **Ability to pay** — for monetary sanctions, the financial burden on the
   sanctioned party/attorney [VERIFY-CITE]

### Sanctions the Court May Impose

| Sanction Type | Description | When Appropriate |
|--------------|-------------|------------------|
| **Corrective order** | Order to withdraw or amend the offending pleading | First-level response; minimum sanction |
| **Attorney's fees** | Reasonable fees incurred as a result of the violation | When violation necessitated motion practice or additional discovery |
| **Costs** | Litigation costs directly attributable to the violation | Same as fees |
| **Monetary penalty** | Fine payable to the court | Serious or repeated violations |
| **Nonmonetary directive** | Education requirement, filing restrictions, supervision | Pattern violators; alternative to monetary sanctions |
| **Contempt** | For violation of prior sanctions order | Repeat offenders only |

### What Sanctions to Request — Decision Framework

**Conservative approach (recommended for first sanctions motion):**
1. Order to withdraw/amend the offending contentions
2. Attorney's fees and costs for the sanctions motion
3. Leave the door open for additional sanctions if conduct continues

**Moderate approach (for clear, documented pattern):**
1. Order to withdraw/amend
2. Attorney's fees and costs for the motion AND for additional work caused
   by the sanctionable conduct (discovery on baseless defenses, etc.)
3. Nonmonetary directive if warranted

**Aggressive approach (for egregious, repeated violations):**
1. All of the above
2. Monetary penalty to the court
3. Filing restrictions or supervision requirements
4. Referral to disciplinary authorities (separate from sanctions motion —
   do not request in the motion itself)

`[DECISION REQUIRED]` — Senior attorney selects the approach based on case
strategy, relationship considerations, and the severity of the violations.

## Defenses to Sanctions — Anticipate and Address

### 1. "Reasonable Inquiry Was Conducted"

**Opposing argument:** Counsel conducted a reasonable investigation before
filing and the contentions had a basis at that time.

**Response:** Show the specific evidence that contradicts the contention and
demonstrate it was available to counsel at the time of filing (party's own
records, prior admissions, publicly available information).

### 2. "Good Faith Disagreement"

**Opposing argument:** The contentions reflect a good faith disagreement
about the facts or law.

**Response:** Distinguish between genuine disputes (not sanctionable) and
contentions that no reasonable attorney would maintain. A denial of a fact
established by the party's own documents is not a "disagreement" — it is a
failure of reasonable inquiry.

### 3. "Alternative Pleading Under Rule 8(e) / NMRA 1-008(E)"

**Opposing argument:** The inconsistent positions are permitted under
alternative pleading.

**Response:** Alternative pleading permits inconsistent claims or defenses.
It does not permit denials of fact that lack evidentiary basis, nor does it
excuse the failure to conduct reasonable inquiry. FRCP 11 imposes obligations
even on alternative pleadings. [VERIFY-CITE]

### 4. "The Safe Harbor Was Not Properly Complied With"

**Opposing argument:** The movant failed to comply with the 21-day safe
harbor procedure.

**Response:** Document strict compliance (service date, method, 21-day
calculation, proof of service). This is a threshold issue — if the court
finds noncompliance, the motion is denied regardless of merit.

### 5. "Cure Was Offered / Partial Cure"

**Opposing argument:** Counsel offered to cure or did partially cure the
identified deficiencies.

**Response:** Identify specifically what was NOT cured. If the partial cure
was inadequate, explain why the remaining violations still warrant sanctions.

### 6. "Sanctions Are Disproportionate"

**Opposing argument:** The requested sanctions exceed what is necessary for
deterrence.

**Response:** Explain why the requested sanctions are the minimum necessary
to deter repetition. Tie each requested sanction to a specific harm caused
by the violation.

## Appellate Considerations

### Preserving the Record

When drafting the motion, ensure the record supports appellate review:

1. **Specific findings:** Request that the court's order include specific
   findings as to which Rule 11(b) subsections were violated and why
2. **Proportionality:** Demonstrate that the sanctions requested are
   proportionate to the violations (minimum sufficient to deter)
3. **Due process:** Ensure the sanctioned party had notice (safe harbor
   service) and opportunity to respond (briefing schedule)
4. **Factual support:** Every assertion of sanctionable conduct must have
   record support — no unsupported characterizations

### Standards of Review

| Issue | Standard | Court |
|-------|----------|-------|

## Ethical Guardrails

### For the Movant (Our Side)

- **Do not file a sanctions motion for tactical advantage.** The motion
  itself must comply with Rule 11. Filing a baseless sanctions motion is
  itself sanctionable. [VERIFY-CITE]
- **Do not speculate about opposing counsel's subjective motives.** Focus
  on objective deficiencies in the pleading and the record evidence.
- **Do not use inflammatory language.** The motion should be clinical and
  evidence-driven. Let the record speak.
- **Comply strictly with the safe harbor procedure.** Noncompliance is a
  dispositive defense.
- **Consider the relationship costs.** Sanctions motions escalate conflict.
  Senior attorney must approve the strategic decision to file.

### NM Rules of Professional Conduct

- **Rule 16-301:** Meritorious claims and contentions — a lawyer shall not
  bring or defend a proceeding unless there is a basis in law and fact
  that is not frivolous [VERIFY-CITE]
- **Rule 16-304:** Fairness to opposing party and counsel [VERIFY-CITE]
- **Rule 16-308:** Reporting professional misconduct — separate obligation;
  do not conflate with Rule 11 sanctions [VERIFY-CITE]
- **Rule 16-804:** Misconduct — conduct prejudicial to the administration
  of justice [VERIFY-CITE]

**Note:** Disciplinary referral is separate from a sanctions motion.
Do not request disciplinary action in the sanctions motion itself.
If the conduct warrants referral, discuss with senior attorney separately.
