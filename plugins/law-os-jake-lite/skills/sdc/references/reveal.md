# SDC Reference: REVEAL Mode — Controlled Disclosure Planning

## Purpose

When it's strategically time to disclose information, REVEAL mode ensures
maximum impact. Every reveal is deliberate, timed, framed, and sequenced
for optimal effect.

## Input Requirements

1. **What to reveal** — Specific Ledger item(s) by source reference
2. **Why now** — Trigger event or strategic rationale
3. **Audience** — Who will learn this (OC, adjuster, mediator, court, jury)
4. **Timing context** — Current phase, upcoming events, deadlines

Optional:
- AIP profile of target audience
- Current Posture
- Existing Reveal Plan (if this is an update)

## Reveal Timing Framework

### AMBUSH-DEPO — Maximum Surprise at Deposition

**When:** Cross-examination of a defense witness during deposition.

**Setup:**
1. Identify the target fact from Surprise Inventory
2. Prepare the document/exhibit but do NOT pre-mark it
3. Build a question sequence that leads the witness to commit to a
   position BEFORE revealing the contradicting evidence
4. The commitment is critical — without it, the witness can explain away

**Execution sequence:**
1. Establish the topic area with general questions
2. Get the witness to commit to their version
3. Lock the commitment: "So it's your testimony that [X]?"
4. Introduce the document: "I'm going to show you what's been marked as
   Exhibit [N]. Do you recognize this document?"
5. Walk through the document slowly — let the contradiction speak
6. The killer question: "How do you reconcile [your testimony] with
   [this document]?"

**Post-reveal:**
- Update Disclosure Ledger → DISCLOSED-SPECIFIC
- Assess what this reveals about other UNDISCLOSED items (inference chains)
- Decide whether to continue revealing or save remaining items

**Risk:** OC may request a break to regroup. The witness may have an
explanation you didn't anticipate. Have follow-up questions prepared.

### AMBUSH-TRIAL — Maximum Impact Before Jury

**When:** Trial cross-examination.

**Setup:** Same as AMBUSH-DEPO but with higher stakes preparation:
- Mock the cross with Murder Board (PB-10)
- Prepare for every possible explanation
- Have backup exhibits ready
- Time the reveal for maximum jury impact (typically early afternoon
  when jurors are most attentive)

**Note:** Some evidence must be disclosed pre-trial (exhibit lists,
witness lists per scheduling order). AMBUSH-TRIAL works with evidence
that was disclosed on the exhibit list but whose SIGNIFICANCE was not
telegraphed, or with impeachment evidence (which may not require
pre-disclosure depending on jurisdiction and court rules).

### MEDIATION-BOMB — Settlement Pressure at Mediation

**When:** During mediation session, typically in a caucus with the mediator
who then communicates the strength to the defense.

**Setup:**
1. Select 1-3 high-SV items from Surprise Inventory
2. Prepare a concise presentation: document + 2-3 sentence explanation
3. Coordinate with mediator if possible (some mediators prefer advance notice)
4. Time the reveal: after defense's first low offer, or when negotiations stall

**Execution:**
- Present to mediator first (in caucus): "We'd like to share something
  the defense hasn't seen."
- Let the mediator decide how to communicate it
- OR present in joint session if permitted and strategically advantageous

**Key principle:** At mediation, you control what to disclose. Mediation
communications are generally privileged (NM Mediation Procedures Act).
This means the defense can't use your mediation disclosures against you
if the case doesn't settle. This makes mediation the safest environment
for high-impact reveals.

**Post-reveal:** If case doesn't settle, the information is privileged.
But the defense NOW KNOWS — their behavior will change even if they can't
use the specific mediation communication. Update Ledger to INFERRED for
any items they can now deduce.

### MOTION-REVEAL — Controlled Disclosure via Court Filing

**When:** Filing a motion that requires citing evidence or facts.

**Setup:**
1. Determine minimum disclosure needed to win the motion
2. REDLINE the motion draft before filing
3. Consider: sealed filing? In camera submission?
4. If public filing: accept that this becomes DISCLOSED-BY-COURT

**Calibration:**
- MSJ opposition → must disclose enough to create genuine dispute of fact
- MTC → must show relevance and proportionality of discovery sought
- Motion to strike → must show specific defects (can be targeted)
- Daubert challenge → must disclose basis for challenge (methodology)

**Minimize collateral disclosure:** Only cite what you need. Don't attach
entire documents as exhibits when a relevant excerpt suffices.

### DEMAND-LEVERAGE — Settlement Negotiation Reveal

**When:** Demand letter, settlement conference, or negotiation communication.

**Setup:**
1. Select items that demonstrate case strength on contested elements
2. Frame them for maximum valuation impact
3. Coordinate with DC SETTLEMENT-EVAL for damages presentation
4. Coordinate with PB-08 Settlement Negotiator for demand structure

**Calibration by adjuster profile:**
- Algorithm-driven adjuster → structured, data-rich disclosure
- Relationship-driven adjuster → narrative disclosure, emotional impact
- Authority-limited adjuster → disclose enough to justify supervisor
  escalation ("this information suggests the case value exceeds your authority")

### PROGRESSIVE-DRIP — Cumulative Momentum Through Sequenced Reveals

**When:** Over a planned timeline (typically 4-12 weeks pre-mediation).

**Setup:**
1. Select 3-5 items from Surprise Inventory
2. Rank by impact (moderate → highest)
3. Map to disclosure vehicles (discovery supplement, deposition,
   motion, demand letter, mediation)
4. Build a timeline with specific dates and delivery methods

**Sequence design principles:**
- Start with moderate-impact items → builds narrative without showing your hand
- Escalate impact → each disclosure is stronger than the last
- Space disclosures 1-2 weeks apart → allows defense to absorb and worry
- Culminate with strongest item at mediation or in demand letter
- Each disclosure should reference the prior ones → cumulative effect

**Example sequence:**

```
PROGRESSIVE REVEAL PLAN — [Case Name]

Week 1: Supplemental discovery response adding [Fact A]
        → Moderate impact. Establishes we have more than they thought.
        → Ledger update: CFP-F#32 → DISCLOSED-SPECIFIC

Week 3: Deposition of [defense witness] — use [Document B] for impeachment
        → High impact. Shows their witness is unreliable.
        → Ledger update: CFP-F#47 → DISCLOSED-SPECIFIC

Week 5: File motion [type] citing [Facts A + B + new argument]
        → Demonstrates cumulative case strength in public filing.
        → Ledger update: LPB-LAW-UPA-001 → DISCLOSED-BY-COURT

Week 7: Updated demand letter with [Damages C] breakdown
        → High-value disclosure. Shows full damages picture.
        → Ledger update: DVP-MED-FUTURE → DISCLOSED-SPECIFIC

Week 9: Mediation — reveal [Smoking Gun D]
        → Maximum impact. Reserved for final pressure.
        → Ledger update: CFP-F#89 → DISCLOSED-SPECIFIC (to mediator/OC)
```

### PREEMPTIVE — Get Ahead of Compelled Disclosure

**When:** You know discovery will compel disclosure of something you've
been holding. Better to control the narrative than have it dragged out.

**Setup:**
1. Identify which UNDISCLOSED items are at risk of compelled disclosure
   (pending MTC, anticipated discovery requests, court orders)
2. For each: what's the best way to disclose this on OUR terms?
3. Frame the disclosure to minimize damage and maintain narrative control

**Execution:**
- Voluntary supplemental disclosure → shows good faith
- Strategic timing → before a hearing where it might come up
- Framing → contextualize the information in our narrative, not theirs
- Follow up → pair with a strength disclosure to maintain balance

## Impact Maximization Principles

1. **Context before content.** Set up the reveal with context that makes
   the audience understand why this matters before showing them what it is.

2. **Commitment before contradiction.** In depositions and negotiations,
   get the other side to commit to a position before showing evidence
   that contradicts it. The commitment makes the contradiction devastating.

3. **One at a time.** Don't dump everything at once (unless OVERWHELMING
   posture). Each item gets its own moment. Multiple simultaneous reveals
   dilute the impact of each.

4. **Let them react.** After a reveal, STOP TALKING. Let the silence work.
   The target's reaction tells you whether the reveal landed.

5. **Follow up, don't pile on.** After a successful reveal, follow up with
   the implications: "So given this document, how do you explain [X]?"
   Don't immediately reveal the next item — let this one breathe.

6. **Save the best for last.** In a sequence, the final reveal should be
   the strongest. This is what they'll remember and what drives their
   decision-making.

7. **Protect the rest.** Every reveal risks inference of other hidden items.
   Before executing, run the inference chain analysis. If revealing Item A
   compromises Items B, C, and D, the cost may exceed the benefit.
