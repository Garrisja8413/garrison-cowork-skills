# SDC Reference: BLUFF Mode — Ethical Bluff Strategy Design

## Purpose

Design ethically permissible bluffs — strategic ambiguity, posturing, and
misdirection within the bounds of zealous advocacy. Every strategy includes
an ethical analysis and requires senior attorney approval.

## Ethical Foundation

**NM RPC 16-301:** A lawyer is an advocate. Advocacy includes presenting
the client's position in its most favorable light. Negotiation is inherently
adversarial — neither side is expected to reveal its bottom line, weaknesses,
or complete hand.

**NM RPC 16-401 (Comment 2):** "Under generally accepted conventions in
negotiation, certain types of statements ordinarily are not taken as
statements of material fact. Estimates of price or value placed on the
subject of a transaction and a party's intentions as to an acceptable
settlement of a claim are ordinarily in this category."

**Key distinction:** Misrepresenting FACTS to the court = prohibited.
Posturing about VALUE, STRENGTH, and INTENTIONS in negotiation = permitted.

## Bluff Type Playbooks

### 1. STRENGTH-PROJECTION

**What it is:** Projecting greater overall case strength than current PCM
gap analysis shows. Making the defense believe you're further along, better
supported, and more dangerous than you actually are right now.

**When to use:**
- Settlement negotiations when PCM has gaps but they're closeable
- Pre-mediation when you want maximum opening demand credibility
- When defense is evaluating whether to make a real offer

**How to execute:**
- Lead with your strongest elements (PCM COVERED status)
- Reference evidence categories broadly: "our medical evidence demonstrates..."
  (even if you have one strong record and two pending)
- Cite admissions aggressively — admissions are undeniable
- Reference "additional evidence" or "witnesses" in general terms
- Use momentum events: "particularly in light of the recent [event]..."
- Do NOT make specific factual claims you can't support

**Example language:**
- "We are confident in our ability to prove each element at trial"
  (permitted even if some elements are LAW-ONLY or FACTS-ONLY — you
  believe discovery will fill gaps)
- "Our damages evidence is substantial" (permitted — it's an opinion
  about value)
- "We have multiple witnesses who can speak to the defendant's conduct"
  (permitted if true, even if you'd prefer more)

**Ethical line:** You CAN project confidence you partially feel. You CANNOT
claim you have evidence you don't have. "We're confident" ≠ "We have proof."

### 2. THEORY-DECOY

**What it is:** Drawing attention to one legal theory while quietly
building another. The defense prepares for the wrong fight.

**When to use:**
- Early-to-mid discovery when you've identified a strong secondary theory
  (e.g., UPA, bad faith) that the defense hasn't anticipated
- When your Complaint is broad enough to support multiple theories
- When you want the defense to spend resources on a less important theory

**How to execute:**
- Complaint: Plead multiple theories. Emphasize Theory A in the
  allegations. Theory B is technically pled but not featured.
- Discovery: Serve requests targeting Theory A prominently. Include
  Theory B requests mixed in, framed as background/routine.
- Depositions: Spend time on Theory A. Ask Theory B questions as
  "follow-ups" or "for background."
- Meanwhile: Build Theory B privately through independent investigation,
  non-party discovery, expert analysis.

**Ethical line:** All theories must be pled in good faith (NMRA 1-011).
This isn't about frivolous theories — it's about emphasis allocation.

### 3. READINESS-BLUFF

**What it is:** Projecting trial readiness to create settlement urgency
when you'd actually prefer to settle.

**When to use:**
- When defense is stalling, hoping you'll fold
- Pre-trial conference phase
- When trial date is approaching and you want maximum pressure

**How to execute:**
- File trial-ready motions in limine
- Disclose expert witnesses (even if engagement isn't final)
- Request pre-trial conference dates aggressively
- Reference trial preparation activities in correspondence
- Prepare (and let OC see) exhibit lists, witness lists, jury instructions
- Schedule deposition of their key witness close to trial

**Example language:**
- "We look forward to presenting this case to a jury"
- "We have completed our trial preparation and are ready to proceed"
  (permitted if you've done substantive prep, even if not fully complete)
- "We intend to call [expert] at trial" (permitted if you've disclosed
  the expert, even if report isn't final)

**Ethical line:** You must actually be willing to try the case. Bluffing
trial readiness when you genuinely cannot or will not try the case may
cross the line into misrepresentation of intentions.

### 4. NUMBERS-ANCHOR

**What it is:** Demanding well above your realistic expected value to
anchor the negotiation range in your favor.

**When to use:**
- Initial demand letter
- Mediation opening
- Any settlement negotiation

**How to execute:**
- Use DC DAMAGES mode to calculate best-case jury value
- Add punitives multiplier if supported
- Use highest comparable from Outcomes Pack
- Present the number as "case value" not "what we'll accept"
- Support with detailed damages breakdown (makes the number look analytical)
- Let the adjuster/OC negotiate you "down" to your actual target

**Example language:**
- "Based on our analysis of comparable verdicts and the severity of our
  client's injuries, we value this case at $[high number]"
- "Our demand of $[X] reflects the minimum we believe a jury would award"

**Ethical line:** This is universally understood as negotiation posturing.
RPC Comment explicitly carves out "estimates of price or value" and
"intentions as to an acceptable settlement." You're in the clear.

### 5. DISCOVERY-FEINT

**What it is:** Discovery requests that suggest pursuit of one theory
while you're actually building another. The defense objects and responds
to the decoy; the real theory develops undisturbed.

**When to use:**
- When you want to build Theory B while the defense focuses on Theory A
- When you want to waste opposing counsel's time on unproductive objections
- When you need to create a discovery record for later motions

**How to execute:**
- Serve requests that prominently target Theory A topics
- Include Theory B requests but frame them as routine or background
- Use broad categories that cover Theory B without highlighting it
- Let the defense object to Theory A requests (creates GFL/MTC issues
  that consume their attention)

**Ethical line:** All requests must seek relevant information (NMRA 1-026).
You cannot serve discovery for harassment or burden alone (NMRA 1-026(G)).
But you CAN prioritize your requests strategically and frame them to
emphasize or de-emphasize different topics.

### 6. WITNESS-IMPLICATION

**What it is:** Implying you have additional testimony available without
specifying who or what, creating uncertainty for the defense.

**When to use:**
- Settlement negotiations
- Mediation
- When defense is evaluating their exposure

**How to execute:**
- "We have identified additional witnesses who will testify regarding
  [topic]" (permitted if you've identified them, even if not yet deposed)
- "Our investigation has revealed testimony supporting [allegation]"
- Reference witness categories: "healthcare providers", "coworkers",
  "treating physicians" — broad enough to be true, vague enough to
  create uncertainty

**Ethical line:** You MUST actually have witnesses or leads. You CANNOT
imply witnesses exist when they don't. The bluff is in the FRAMING (how
many, how strong), not in the EXISTENCE.

### 7. TIMELINE-PRESSURE

**What it is:** Using scheduling and deadlines to create urgency that
pressures the defense into action.

**How to execute:**
- Request early trial settings
- Set tight cure deadlines in good faith letters
- Schedule depositions strategically (bunch them to create workload pressure)
- File motions with short response windows (where rules permit)
- Reference approaching deadlines in settlement communications

### 8. SILENCE-AS-STRENGTH

**What it is:** Strategic non-response that lets the defense's own fear
and uncertainty fill the void. The unknown is often more frightening than
the known.

**When to use:**
- After sending a strong demand — let it sit
- After a favorable ruling — don't explain what's next
- When defense makes a low offer — silence says more than words
- After obtaining powerful evidence — don't rush to use it

**How to execute:**
- Send the demand/motion/letter. Then stop communicating.
- Do NOT respond to "just checking in" emails from OC
- Do NOT explain your strategy or next steps
- Let the deadline expire (or nearly expire) before responding
- When you do respond, be brief and confident

**Maintenance:** The hardest bluff to maintain because it requires doing
nothing. Set a calendar reminder for the planned response date and do
not break silence before then.

## Multi-Bluff Combinations

Complex litigation often calls for layered bluffs:

**"The Full Press"** (pre-mediation):
1. NUMBERS-ANCHOR (high demand letter)
2. READINESS-BLUFF (trial motions in limine)
3. WITNESS-IMPLICATION (reference additional witnesses)
4. SILENCE-AS-STRENGTH (demand sent, no follow-up)
5. REVEAL at mediation (selective disclosure of strongest surprise items)

**"The Misdirection"** (mid-discovery):
1. THEORY-DECOY (emphasize Theory A in discovery)
2. DISCOVERY-FEINT (heavy discovery on Theory A topics)
3. Build Theory B through non-party subpoenas and investigation
4. REVEAL Theory B via Amended Complaint when B is fully built

**"The Squeeze"** (post-discovery):
1. STRENGTH-PROJECTION (cite your best evidence prominently)
2. TIMELINE-PRESSURE (early trial setting, aggressive scheduling)
3. PROGRESSIVE reveal of additional evidence items
4. NUMBERS-ANCHOR (escalating demand tied to new disclosures)

## Bluff Maintenance & Risk Register

Every active bluff gets tracked:

```
| BluffID | Type | Started | Target | Status | Maintenance-Action | Risk-Level | Next-Review |
```

**Status values:**
- `ACTIVE` — bluff is running
- `CALLED` — target challenged the bluff; execute exit strategy
- `CONVERTED` — bluff became real (we achieved the position we projected)
- `ABANDONED` — bluff no longer serves strategic purpose
- `EXPOSED` — bluff was discovered; damage assessment needed

**Risk Levels:**
- `LOW` — fully within RPC bounds, minimal reputational risk
- `MODERATE` — within RPC but could damage credibility if exposed
- `HIGH` — gray zone; senior attorney oversight required
- `CRITICAL` — approaching ethical lines; consider abandoning

## Ethical Compliance Checklist (Every Bluff)

Before generating any Bluff Strategy Card, verify:

- [ ] No fabrication of evidence (actual or implied)
- [ ] No false statements about material facts
- [ ] No misrepresentation to the court
- [ ] All discovery obligations remain honored
- [ ] Strategy falls within NM RPC 16-301 advocacy bounds
- [ ] Value/intention statements qualify under RPC Comment 2 carve-out
- [ ] Senior attorney approval flagged (`[DECISION REQUIRED]`)
- [ ] Exit strategy defined in case bluff is called
- [ ] Reputational risk assessed

**If ANY box cannot be checked → DO NOT GENERATE the Bluff Strategy Card.**
Instead, output: "This strategy raises ethical concerns under [rule].
Recommend alternative approach: [suggestion]."
