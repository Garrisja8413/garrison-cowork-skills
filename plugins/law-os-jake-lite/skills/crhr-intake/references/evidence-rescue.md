# Phase 5: First 48 Hours Action Items — The "Evidence Rescue"

## Purpose

Force the government to hand over internal records and preserve video evidence
before it is "accidentally" deleted, overwritten, or lost. The first 48 hours
after case acceptance are the most critical window for evidence preservation in
civil rights cases.

## Step 12: Launch IPRA Requests (Inspection of Public Records Act)

### The IPRA Advantage

New Mexico's Inspection of Public Records Act (NMSA §14-2-1 et seq.) is one of
the most powerful public records laws in the country. Unlike private litigation
where you must wait for formal discovery, IPRA allows you to force government
agencies to produce records immediately — they legally have **15 business days**
to respond.

### IPRA Request Categories

Send separate, targeted IPRA requests for each category to maximize compliance
and minimize delay tactics:

#### Request 1: Video Evidence (HIGHEST PRIORITY)

| Item | Specifics |
|------|----------|
| Bodycam/OBRD footage | All unedited, native-format files from every officer present |
| Dashcam footage | In-car camera recordings from every patrol unit at scene |
| Facility CCTV (jail cases) | All pod cameras, hallway cameras, intake cameras, booking cameras |
| Drone/aerial footage | If any aerial surveillance was deployed |
| Interview room recordings | If client was interrogated at the station |

**Critical specification:** Request "unedited, native files" — not
redacted summaries or selected clips. Agencies will try to produce
curated excerpts that omit the most damaging footage.

#### Request 2: Dispatch & Communications

| Item | Specifics |
|------|----------|
| CAD (Computer-Aided Dispatch) reports | Complete CAD log for the incident |
| 911 audio recordings | All 911 calls related to the incident |
| Radio transmissions | Officer radio traffic during the incident |
| MDT (Mobile Data Terminal) messages | In-car computer messages between officers |

#### Request 3: Official Reports & Documentation

| Item | Specifics |
|------|----------|
| Primary police report / incident report | Complete report with all supplements |
| Use of Force report | Mandatory filing when force is used |
| Supervisor review of force | Required review of every UOF incident |
| Internal Affairs complaint (if filed) | Any IA investigation opened |
| Officer injury report | If officer claims injury during incident |
| Evidence/property log | Chain of custody for any seized items |
| Arrest report / booking sheet | If client was arrested |

#### Request 4: Officer Personnel Records

| Item | Specifics |
|------|----------|
| Disciplinary history | Prior complaints, investigations, sustained findings |
| Use of Force history | Officer's pattern of force incidents |
| Training records | Use of force training, de-escalation training |
| Early Warning System alerts | If agency uses an early intervention system |
| Prior lawsuits / claims | Cases naming this officer as defendant |

**Note:** Agencies frequently resist producing personnel records. Be prepared
to appeal denials. Under NM law, disciplinary records of public employees are
generally public records subject to IPRA.

#### Request 5: Jail/Prison-Specific Records (If Applicable)

| Item | Specifics |
|------|----------|
| Pod CCTV footage | ALL camera angles in the relevant housing unit |
| Cell-door electronic logs | Automated logs showing when doors were opened/closed |
| Taser deployment logs | Electronic records from each Taser device used |
| Guard shift rosters | Who was on duty during the incident |
| Medical request kites | Client's requests for medical attention |
| Grievance records | All grievances filed by client |
| Incident reports | Staff incident reports for the event |
| Use of force logs | Facility UOF documentation |
| Classification records | Client's housing classification and history |
| Inmate communication records | Phone calls, emails, visits logged |

### Handling IPRA Delays and Denials

Police departments frequently delay IPRA requests using these tactics:

| Tactic | Counter |
|--------|---------|
| "Ongoing criminal investigation" exemption | Challenge: NMSA §14-2-1(A) requires balancing test; criminal investigation does not automatically exempt all records. Appeal to NM AG. |
| "Pending litigation" claim | Challenge: IPRA applies regardless of pending litigation. Records are public; litigation doesn't change their status. |
| Extended response time (beyond 15 days) | File complaint with NM Department of Justice, IPRA enforcement division |
| Producing only partial/redacted records | Follow up demanding unredacted native files; cite that redaction must be justified record-by-record |
| Claiming records don't exist | Demand a search certification; if bodycam was required by policy, the absence of footage is itself evidence |
| Excessive copying fees | NM law limits fees to reasonable actual costs; challenge excessive fees |

### IPRA Denial Appeal Process

1. Send written appeal to the agency's chief administrative officer
2. If denied again, file complaint with the **NM Attorney General's Office**,
   Open Government Division
3. Consider filing a civil enforcement action under NMSA §14-2-12
4. Document every delay — IPRA obstruction can become independent evidence
   of the agency's bad faith

## Step 13: Send Spoliation (Preservation) Letters

### Purpose

A spoliation/preservation letter is a formal, certified demand commanding the
government entity to suspend all automated deletion protocols and preserve
every piece of evidence related to the incident.

### Why This Is Urgent

| Evidence Type | Typical Retention/Overwrite Period |
|--------------|-----------------------------------|
| Jail pod CCTV | 14–30 days (then automatically overwritten) |
| Bodycam/OBRD | 60–180 days (depending on agency policy) |
| Dashcam footage | 60–90 days |
| CAD/dispatch records | 1–3 years |
| Radio transmissions | 30–90 days |
| MDT messages | 30–90 days |
| Taser download data | Varies — may be overwritten with next deployment |
| Cell-door electronic logs | 30–90 days |

### Preservation Letter Recipients

| Recipient | Title | Entity |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Chief of Police | Administrative head | Municipal PD |
| County Sheriff | Elected official | County Sheriff's Office |
| Warden / Administrator | Facility head | Jail / prison |
| City Attorney | Legal counsel | City government |
| County Attorney | Legal counsel | County government |
| Risk Management Division | State risk manager | State agencies (GSD) |
| IT Director | Records custodian | For electronic systems |

### Preservation Demand — Required Content

| Section | Content |
|---------|---------|
| Identification | Client name, incident date, time, location, report # |
| Legal basis | NM spoliation doctrine; *Galindo v. Western States Leasing*, 2019-NMCA-031 |
| Specific items to preserve | Itemized list — every category of evidence |
| Duration | "Until further notice" or "pending resolution of litigation" |
| Warning | Destruction may result in adverse inference jury instruction and sanctions |
| Acknowledgment request | Demand written confirmation of preservation compliance |

### Preservation Letter Template

```
[FIRM LETTERHEAD]

VIA CERTIFIED MAIL — RETURN RECEIPT REQUESTED

[Date]

[Chief of Police / Sheriff / Warden]
[Agency/Facility Name]
[Address]

RE: DEMAND FOR PRESERVATION OF EVIDENCE — LITIGATION HOLD
    Incident Date: [Date]
    Incident Location: [Location]
    Report Number: [If known]
    Client: [Name]

Dear [Title] [Name]:

This firm represents [Client Name] in connection with an incident that
occurred on [date] at [location] involving [officer names / staff, if
known]. We anticipate filing a civil rights lawsuit arising from this
incident.

PRESERVATION DEMAND

You are hereby directed to immediately preserve all evidence related
to this incident, including but not limited to the following:

ELECTRONIC RECORDS:
1. All body-worn camera (OBRD) recordings from every officer/staff
   member present during the incident
2. All dashcam/in-car camera recordings from every vehicle at the scene
3. All facility CCTV/surveillance recordings from [date, time range]
   covering [specific areas]
4. All CAD (Computer-Aided Dispatch) records
5. All 911 audio recordings
6. All radio transmissions
7. All MDT (Mobile Data Terminal) messages
8. All cell-door electronic access logs
9. All Taser/CEW deployment data downloads
10. All GPS/AVL data from patrol vehicles

DOCUMENTS:
11. All police/incident reports, supplements, and narratives
12. All Use of Force reports
13. All supervisor review documents
14. All Internal Affairs files related to this incident
15. All officer/staff disciplinary records for involved personnel
16. All training records for involved personnel
17. All guard shift rosters for [date]
18. All medical request records for [Client Name]
19. All grievance records filed by [Client Name]
20. All inmate classification and housing records

You are directed to suspend all automated deletion, overwriting, or
recycling protocols that would affect any of the above categories of
evidence. This includes, but is not limited to, suspending video
surveillance system overwrite schedules, email deletion policies, and
electronic records purging routines.

LEGAL CONSEQUENCES OF SPOLIATION

Under New Mexico law, the intentional or negligent destruction of
relevant evidence gives rise to an adverse inference instruction,
permitting the jury to presume the destroyed evidence was unfavorable
to your client. See Galindo v. Western States Leasing, 2019-NMCA-031;
see also Torres v. El Paso Elec. Co., 1999-NMSC-029, ¶ 28.

Federal courts impose even harsher sanctions for spoliation under
Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(e), including case-dispositive sanctions for
intentional destruction.

Please confirm receipt of this letter and your compliance with this
preservation demand in writing within ten (10) days.

[Attorney Signature Block]
```

## Step 14: File Administrative Charges (If Applicable)

### When Administrative Charges Are Required

If this is a human rights / employment discrimination case, the paralegal
must draft and file formal charges of discrimination to preserve the right
to sue in federal court.

### Cross-Filing Protocol

File with BOTH agencies simultaneously — they have a work-sharing agreement:

| Agency | Filing Deadline | Jurisdiction | Purpose |
|--------|----------------|--------------|---------|
| **New Mexico Human Rights Bureau (NMHRB)** | 300 days from discriminatory act | NM state claims (NMHRA) | Preserve state discrimination claims |
| **Federal EEOC** | 300 days from discriminatory act (in NM, a deferral state) | Federal claims (Title VII, ADA, ADEA) | Secure "Right to Sue" letter for federal court |

### Charge of Discrimination — Required Content

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Complainant information | Client name, address, phone, email |
| Respondent information | Employer/landlord/business name, address, phone, contact person |
| Basis of discrimination | Protected class (race, sex, age, disability, etc.) |
| Date(s) of discrimination | Specific dates of each discriminatory act |
| Description of discriminatory acts | Narrative of what happened |
| Comparator information | Who was treated more favorably and why |
| Harm suffered | Lost wages, emotional distress, other damages |

### Right to Sue Letter Process

1. After filing the charge, the EEOC investigates (or defers to NMHRB)
2. Client can request a "Right to Sue" letter at any time after 180 days
3. Once issued, client has **90 days** to file a federal lawsuit
4. The NMHRB process is separate — client can file in NM state court under
   the NMHRA without waiting for the Right to Sue letter
