# Inference Standards — NM Legal Framework

## Summary Judgment Standard for Inferences

### Nonmovant's Favorable Inferences
At summary judgment, the court must view the evidence in the light most
favorable to the nonmoving party and draw all reasonable inferences in
the nonmovant's favor.

- **NMRA 1-056** — Summary judgment standard `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- NM case law: "All reasonable inferences from the evidence must be drawn
  in favor of the party opposing the motion." `[VERIFY-CITE]`

### What Makes an Inference "Reasonable"

An inference is reasonable when:
1. It follows logically from established facts
2. A rational factfinder could draw it from the evidence presented
3. It does not require speculation, conjecture, or guesswork
4. It is not contradicted by undisputed evidence in the record

An inference is NOT reasonable when:
1. It requires a speculative leap unsupported by the evidence
2. Multiple inferential steps are required with no supporting evidence
   at each step
3. The undisputed evidence compels the opposite conclusion
4. It is based on possibility rather than probability

`[VERIFY-CITE]` for NM-specific holdings on each point.

### The "Scintilla" Rule

A mere scintilla of evidence is insufficient to create a genuine dispute.
The nonmovant must present evidence from which a reasonable factfinder
could return a verdict in their favor. `[VERIFY-CITE]`

### Inference Upon Inference

NM law on stacked inferences (inference drawn from another inference):

- Generally disfavored — weakens the chain
- Not categorically prohibited
- Each link in the chain must be independently reasonable
- The overall chain must not amount to speculation
- A chain of two strong inferences may be permissible
- A chain of three or more weak inferences is likely speculative

## Trial Standard for Inferences

### Jury Instructions on Inferences

- UJI 13-202 NMRA — Circumstantial evidence and inferences `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- Jury may draw reasonable inferences from proven facts
- Jury is not required to draw any particular inference
- Circumstantial evidence is not inherently less reliable than direct evidence

### Burden of Proof and Inferences

- Plaintiff's burden: preponderance of evidence (more likely than not)
- An inference satisfies the burden if it is more probable than not
- Multiple reasonable inferences: if plaintiff's is equally or more probable,
  burden is met
- Defendant's alternative inference: if equally probable, plaintiff fails

## Spoliation Inferences

When a party destroys or fails to preserve evidence:

- The factfinder may infer the missing evidence was unfavorable to the
  spoliating party
- NM follows a multi-factor test for spoliation sanctions `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- Factors: duty to preserve, degree of culpability, relevance of destroyed
  evidence, prejudice to opposing party

## Medical Inferences

For medical malpractice and personal injury:

- Causation inferences typically require expert testimony
- Res ipsa loquitur permits inference of negligence from the event itself
  when: (1) event ordinarily does not occur without negligence, (2) instrumentality
  was in defendant's exclusive control, (3) plaintiff did not contribute `[VERIFY-CITE]`
- "Loss of chance" doctrine — inference that delayed diagnosis/treatment
  reduced probability of better outcome `[VERIFY-CITE]`

## Insurance Inferences

For insurance coverage disputes:

- Ambiguity in policy → inference that coverage exists (contra proferentem)
- Insurer's failure to investigate → inference of bad faith
- Insurer's internal documents showing knowledge of coverage → inference
  of unreasonable denial
