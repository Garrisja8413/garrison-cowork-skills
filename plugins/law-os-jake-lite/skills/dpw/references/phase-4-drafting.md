# DPW Phase 4: Demand Letter Drafting — Reference

## Purpose

Write a highly persuasive, structured document tailored to exploit the specific
legal vulnerabilities of the defendant. Phase 4 applies practice-area-specific
drafting tactics, defines the universal demand letter anatomy, and includes the
critical bad faith trigger language that transforms the demand from a mere
settlement request into a legal weapon.

Phase 4 has three steps:
- Step 8: Practice-Area Specific Drafting Tactics
- Step 9: Anatomy of the Demand Letter
- Step 10: The Bad Faith Trigger

**DPW routes to the Demand skill for the actual letter body.** Phase 4 provides
the strategic framework and tactical parameters; the Demand skill produces the
BODY-ONLY text per its own Pack-First, Direct-Output architecture.

## Part 1: Practice-Area Specific Drafting Tactics (Step 8)

### 1.1 Personal Injury — The "Colossus" Format

**Target audience:** Insurance adjusters using AI-driven severity scoring
software (Colossus, ClaimIQ, or similar systems).

**Strategic insight:** Major carriers (State Farm, Allstate, GEICO, Progressive)
use algorithmic claim-evaluation systems that parse demand letters for specific
medical terminology and treatment keywords. If the demand does not feed the
system the exact keywords it scores, the algorithm undervalues the claim and
the adjuster has "software-backed" authority to lowball.

**Mandatory keyword categories to include:**

| Keyword Category | Examples to Use in Narrative | Why It Scores |
|-----------------|------------------------------|---------------|
| **Pain descriptors** | "muscle spasm," "radiating pain," "burning sensation," "sharp stabbing pain" | Severity scoring weight |
| **Diagnostic terms** | "radiculopathy," "disc herniation," "stenosis," "neuropathy" | Diagnostic complexity score |
| **Impairment language** | "permanent impairment rating," "functional limitations," "maximum medical improvement" | Permanence multiplier |
| **ADL impact** | "unable to perform activities of daily living," "cannot lift more than 10 pounds," "requires assistance with bathing/dressing" | Disability impact score |
| **Treatment intensity** | "epidural steroid injection," "surgical intervention," "hardware implantation," "nerve block" | Treatment severity weight |
| **Prognosis terms** | "guarded prognosis," "chronic condition," "degenerative progression," "lifetime management" | Future damages multiplier |

**Drafting format for Colossus optimization:**
- Use medical terminology precisely (match CPT code descriptions)
- Present each body part injury separately with its own treatment timeline
- Quantify pain frequency and duration ("constant," "daily," "3-4 times per week")
- State specific functional limitations with measurable restrictions
- Reference the treating physician's exact language from medical records
- Include permanence language from the final treating physician or IME

### 1.2 Medical Malpractice — The Science & The PR Threat

**Target audience:** Hospital risk management, medical malpractice carrier,
defense counsel.

**Strategic insight:** Med mal demands must lead with unimpeachable science.
The demand is an opportunity for the defendant doctor/hospital to settle quietly.
The alternative — a filed Medical Review Commission (MRC) application — is a
matter of public record that exposes the provider to media scrutiny, NPDB
reporting, and potential hospital credentialing action.

**Drafting tactics:**

| Tactic | Implementation |
|--------|---------------|
| **Lead with the expert** | Quote the reviewing expert extensively. Name their credentials, board certifications, publications. The expert IS the demand. |
| **Standard of care as simple as possible** | Reduce the standard of care violation to its simplest formulation: "A reasonably competent [specialty] would have [done X]. Dr. [Name] failed to [do X]." |
| **Causation chain** | Walk step-by-step from the breach to the injury. Use "but for" causation language: "But for Dr. [Name]'s failure to [X], the patient would not have suffered [Y]." |
| **Frame as opportunity** | "This demand provides [Hospital/Doctor] an opportunity to resolve this matter confidentially, before a Medical Review Commission application is filed which would become a matter of public record." |
| **PR pressure (subtle)** | Reference the NPDB (National Practitioner Data Bank) reporting requirement. Note that MRC applications trigger hospital credentialing review. Do not threaten media directly — the implication is sufficient. |
| **Cite medical literature** | When the standard of care is established in published guidelines (e.g., AHA, ACOG, AAOS), cite the specific guideline. This makes the breach objective, not just one expert's opinion. |

### 1.3 Civil Rights / Human Rights — The Fee-Shifting Threat

**Target audience:** Government entity (city, county, state) risk management,
government defense counsel.

**Strategic insight:** The biggest leverage in civil rights cases is NOT the
damages — it is the **statutory attorney's fees**. Under the NM Civil Rights
Act (NMSA §41-4A-12) and 42 USC §1988 (for federal §1983 claims), a prevailing
plaintiff recovers reasonable attorney's fees. For a complex civil rights case,
these fees can dwarf the damages award. The government entity is paying for
YOUR firm's time if they lose.

Additionally, the NM Civil Rights Act **bans qualified immunity** (NMSA
§41-4A-4). This means the government cannot hide behind the traditional defense
that shields individual officers. Trial exposure is guaranteed.

**Drafting tactics:**

| Tactic | Implementation |
|--------|---------------|
| **Lead with constitutional violation** | Frame the incident as a violation of fundamental rights, not just negligence. Use constitutional language: "unreasonable seizure," "excessive force," "deliberate indifference." |
| **Fee-shifting math** | Explicitly state: "Under [statute], a prevailing plaintiff recovers reasonable attorney's fees. Our firm's hourly billing rate is $[X]/hour. [X] attorneys and [X] paralegals have invested [X] hours to date. Projected through trial, attorney's fees alone will exceed $[amount]." |
| **No QI reminder** | "Under the New Mexico Civil Rights Act, NMSA §41-4A-4, qualified immunity is not available as a defense. This case WILL proceed to trial on the merits." |
| **Bodycam/video emphasis** | "The [City/County]'s own bodycam footage, obtained through IPRA, documents [specific constitutional violations]. A jury will see this footage." |
| **Pattern and practice** | If IPRA/FOIA records reveal prior complaints or similar incidents, reference them: "Records reveal [X] prior complaints against [Officer/Department] for similar conduct, establishing a pattern of [constitutional violation]." |
| **Municipal liability** | For Monell claims: "The [City/County] is directly liable under Monell v. Dep't of Social Services for maintaining policies, customs, and practices that caused [Plaintiff]'s constitutional injuries." |

### 1.4 Consumer Protection — The Treble Threat

**Target audience:** Corporate defendant, corporate counsel, liability carrier.

**Strategic insight:** Consumer protection statutes are punitive by design.
The NM Unfair Practices Act (NMSA §57-12-10) provides treble (triple) damages
for willful violations AND mandatory attorney's fee shifting. For a defendant
facing a well-documented pattern of fraud, the exposure is catastrophic:
(actual damages × 3) + attorney's fees + costs + punitive damages.

**Drafting tactics:**

| Tactic | Implementation |
|--------|---------------|
| **Cite the statute precisely** | "Under NMSA §57-12-10(B), because [Defendant]'s conduct was willful, the Court shall award treble damages." Use "shall" — it is mandatory, not discretionary. |
| **Itemize each violation** | List every statutory violation separately. Each may carry its own damages and multiplier. |
| **Willfulness evidence** | Document evidence of willfulness: prior complaints, internal communications showing knowledge, pattern across multiple consumers. "Willful" means the defendant knew or should have known the conduct was unfair/deceptive. |
| **Treble math** | Show the math explicitly: "Actual damages: $[X]. Treble damages (mandatory for willful violation): $[X × 3]. Attorney's fees (mandatory): $[X]. Total exposure: $[total]." |
| **Class action threat** | If the fraud affects multiple consumers: "Our investigation indicates [Defendant] engaged in this practice with [X] other consumers. This demand addresses only our client's individual claim. [Defendant] should be aware of its broader exposure." |
| **Regulatory referral** | "We are also considering referring this matter to the New Mexico Attorney General's Consumer Protection Division for investigation of violations of the Unfair Practices Act." |

## Part 2: Anatomy of the Demand Letter (Step 9)

Every demand letter, regardless of practice area, follows this structural
framework. Practice-area tactics from Part 1 are applied within each section.

### Section 1: Introduction & The Deadline

**Content:**
- State who you represent (merge tokens for client name)
- Reference the claim number, policy number, or case reference
- State the strict response deadline (typically 15 to 30 days)
- Professional tone: firm but not hostile

**Template pattern:**
```
This firm represents [CLIENT] regarding injuries sustained [on DATE /
arising from INCIDENT DESCRIPTION] involving [DEFENDANT/INSURED].
This matter is referenced under Claim Number [#] / Policy Number [#].

This demand constitutes a formal offer of settlement. A response is
required no later than [DEADLINE DATE] at 5:00 PM [TIMEZONE].
```

### Section 2: Liability Narrative (Paint the Villain)

**Content:**
- Tell a story of gross negligence, constitutional malice, or willful fraud
- Highlight aggravating factors that make the defendant's conduct indefensible
- Use specific, vivid details from CFP facts (with Fact# references)
- Legal standard from LPB (duty, breach, causation)

**Aggravating factors to highlight (if present in CFP):**

| Factor | Impact |
|--------|--------|
| Speeding / texting / intoxicated | Gross negligence → punitive damages argument |
| Ignoring prior complaints | Pattern of disregard → corporate liability |
| Laughing / mocking on bodycam | Deliberate indifference → constitutional malice |
| Destroying evidence | Spoliation inference → adverse presumption |
| Prior similar incidents | Notice → foreseeability → heightened duty |
| Policy violations by employee | Respondeat superior + negligent supervision |

### Section 3: The Medical / Damage Chronology (Paint the Victim)

**Content:**
- Walk through the treatment timeline chronologically
- Highlight invasive, painful procedures with vivid (but accurate) description
- Use specific medical terminology that scores in carrier algorithms
- Every medical fact traces to a CFP row and DVP billing entry

**Vivid description technique:**
Do NOT write: "Patient received epidural steroid injections."
DO write: "Ms. [Name] endured three epidural steroid injections, each consisting
of a thick needle driven into her lumbar spine under fluoroscopic guidance, in
an attempt to manage the radiating pain caused by the [defendant]'s negligence."

The description must be **accurate** — every detail must come from the medical
records. But the presentation should make the reader see and feel the treatment.

### Section 4: The Human Toll

**Content:**
- How did the injuries affect the client's daily life?
- Impact on sleep, marriage, hobbies, career, parenting
- Before/after comparison using specific examples from client declarations
- Mental health impact (anxiety, depression, PTSD, nightmares)

**Structure:**
- **Before:** "Prior to this incident, [Client] was [description of active,
  productive life]."
- **After:** "Since the incident, [Client] has been unable to [specific
  activities]. [Client] now requires [specific assistance]. [Client]'s
  marriage has suffered because [specific impact]."

### Section 5: The Itemized Financial Ledger

**Content:**
- A clean, bolded table of all damages categories
- Past medicals, future medicals, lost wages, out-of-pocket, general damages
- The final global monetary demand amount
- Every dollar amount traces to DC output and DVP pack

**Table format:**

```
ITEMIZED DAMAGES SUMMARY
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
PAST MEDICAL EXPENSES:
  [Provider 1]:                    $XX,XXX.XX
  [Provider 2]:                    $XX,XXX.XX
  [Provider N]:                    $XX,XXX.XX
  SUBTOTAL PAST MEDICALS:         $XX,XXX.XX

FUTURE MEDICAL EXPENSES:
  [Per Life Care Plan / Expert]:   $XX,XXX.XX

PAST LOST WAGES:                   $XX,XXX.XX
FUTURE LOST EARNING CAPACITY:      $XX,XXX.XX
OUT-OF-POCKET EXPENSES:            $XX,XXX.XX

NON-ECONOMIC DAMAGES:
  Pain, suffering, mental anguish, loss of enjoyment of life,
  disfigurement, loss of consortium, and other UJI 13-1801
  through 13-1830 categories.

STATUTORY ENHANCEMENTS:
  [Treble damages / fee-shifting / punitive, if applicable]
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
TOTAL DEMAND:                     $XXX,XXX.XX
─────────────────────────────────────────────────
```

### Section 6: [Handled by Demand Skill Sections 5-6]

Comparable outcomes (if DVP available) and demand amount/deadline are produced
by the Demand skill per its output contract.

### Section 7: Enclosures List

**Content:**
- Every exhibit enclosed with the demand package, referenced by Bates number
- Organized by category (medical records, bills, expert reports, photos, etc.)

## Part 3: The Bad Faith Trigger (Step 10)

### 3.1 Purpose

The bad faith trigger language is the most legally significant paragraph in the
demand letter. It transforms the demand from a negotiation document into
evidence for a potential bad faith claim. If the carrier fails to respond
reasonably, this language becomes Exhibit A in the bad faith lawsuit.

### 3.2 Required Bad Faith Trigger Language

**Every demand MUST include language substantially similar to:**

```
This demand provides [CARRIER NAME] a reasonable opportunity to protect
[INSURED NAME] from a devastating excess judgment. The facts of this
case establish clear liability and damages that [meet/far exceed] the
available policy limits.

If this demand is not accepted in full by [DEADLINE DATE] at 5:00 PM
[TIMEZONE], it will be permanently withdrawn and will not be renewed
or extended. We will immediately file suit and will hold [CARRIER NAME]
strictly liable for bad faith failure to settle within policy limits
under the NM Unfair Claims Practices Act (NMSA §59A-16-20) and common
law bad faith (Sloan v. State Farm, 2004-NMSC-004).

[CARRIER NAME] is reminded of its duty under NM law to:
  (a) conduct a prompt and reasonable investigation;
  (b) evaluate the claim in good faith;
  (c) make a reasonable settlement offer when liability is clear; and
  (d) not compel the insured to litigate to recover amounts due.

This demand constitutes notice of [CARRIER NAME]'s exposure to
extracontractual liability, including emotional distress damages,
punitive damages, and attorney's fees, should it fail to act
reasonably on this claim.
```

### 3.3 Bad Faith Trigger Rules

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Deadline must be specific** | Date AND time. "30 days" is ambiguous — use "March 15, 2026 at 5:00 PM MST." |
| **"Permanently withdrawn"** | The demand is dead after the deadline. This creates urgency and removes the adjuster's incentive to delay. |
| **Cite the statute** | Reference NMSA §59A-16-20 (Unfair Claims Practices Act) by section number. |
| **Cite Sloan** | Sloan v. State Farm, 2004-NMSC-004, is the foundational NM bad faith case. |
| **List the duties** | Enumerate the specific duties the carrier must fulfill. Each unfulfilled duty becomes a separate basis for bad faith. |
| **Extracontractual exposure** | State explicitly that bad faith exposes the carrier to damages beyond policy limits. |
| **Follow through** | If the deadline passes without acceptable resolution, FILE SUIT. The bad faith trigger is worthless if the firm bluffs. |

### 3.4 Practice-Area Variations

| Practice Area | Bad Faith Trigger Adaptation |
|--------------|------------------------------|
| **PI / Premises** | Standard language above. Emphasize excess judgment exposure if damages exceed limits. |
| **Med Mal** | Add: "This demand also constitutes pre-suit notice as required under the Medical Malpractice Act. If not resolved, we will file a Medical Review Commission application, which will become a matter of public record." |
| **Civil Rights** | Add: "The NM Civil Rights Act, NMSA §41-4A-1 et seq., provides no qualified immunity defense. Trial exposure is certain. Attorney's fees under §41-4A-12 are mandatory for a prevailing plaintiff." |
| **Consumer Protection** | Add: "Under NMSA §57-12-10(B), treble damages are mandatory for willful violations. The court has no discretion to reduce this multiplier. [Defendant]'s total exposure is $[3× actual + fees + costs]." |
